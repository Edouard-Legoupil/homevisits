SYRIAN REFUGEES LIVING OUTSIDE CAMPS IN JORDAN

Home Visits Data Findings, 2013

Outside of the the camps: the struggle facing syrian refugees in Jordan's towns and cities

Three years after the start of Syria’s conflict, the refugee crisis shows no signs of abating. Global attention has focused on the massive and expanding camps near Syria’s borders: Jordan’s Zaatari camp – home to 125,000 people – has become one of the most high-­‐profile symbols of the war. Less known, however, is that Zaatari only represents a small portion of Syrian refugees living in Jordan: less than 20 per cent.

450,000 people living outisde of the camps in growing difficulty, despite the outstanding generosity and significant support that Jordan has continued to offer them, including free public health and education. Half of the tens of thousands refugee families surveyed reported living in inadequate accommodation, facing rising rents, many struggling to pay the bills and facing educational challenges for their children.

A landmark study

Launched in 2012 as part of UNHCR’s cash assessment program, the Home Visit project aims to evaluate the effectiveness of the assistance given to Syrian refugees living outside the UNHCR camps.

“Syrian Refugees Living Outside Camps in Jordan” is the outcome of more than a year-­‐long effort to assess their needs and provide help. Over the course of 2012-­‐2013, UNHCR and its longstanding partner International Relief and Development (IRD) conducted interviews with 92,000 households, putting together a compelling snapshot of their daily struggle to survive.

The report provides a detailed picture of the needs, vulnerabilities and capacities of the Syrian refugees living in urban and rural settings.

A Crisis in Rent and Accommodation

Refugee families reported that an average 60 per cent of expenditure goes on rent.

Average rents for refugees are rising across the country, in some cases by more than 25 per cent for Syrians in some locations from 2012 to 2013.
